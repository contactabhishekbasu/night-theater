Workers what am I looking at?

One row per live process. Lane A is the sole acquirer — it politely probes the archive for a night's frames, walks the ±3-min cadence to enumerate the session, and downloads it. Lanes W1…Wn are elastic reducers that each run NASA's EXOTIC pipeline on one night (align frames → nested-sampling fit → score against published values). The pool grows when the Mac is idle (more RAM/quiet CPU) and backs off under load — so it naturally ramps overnight. Each lane's own bar shows where that process is; a stuck worker no longer freezes the whole display. A red dot means no heartbeat for >2 min (the watchdog will requeue its night).

The night, played back explain

Every downloaded frame, played as a film. The blue circle is the target star (the one with the planet); the green circle is the comparison star we measure it against — dividing by the comparison cancels clouds and haze that affect both stars equally. The circles follow the stars as the telescope drifts (measured by phase correlation between frames — the dx/dy readout). The building curve below is a quick-look brightness ratio, not the science result: EXOTIC redoes this properly with optimal apertures and error analysis.

Transit coverage explain

Green ticks are our frames in time; the shaded band is when the planet was predicted to cross its star (computed from the published orbital period). A trustworthy mid-transit measurement needs frames before, during, and after the event — a transit missing its start or end fits with a confidently wrong time (we proved this on test data: the "partial-coverage trap").

Frame quality explain

Sky background per frame: a rising line means moonlight, haze, or dawn; spikes mean passing clouds. Peak ADU near the sensor maximum means saturation — photometry on a saturated star is unusable, which is why the pipeline checks it.

Last fitted light curve

Science card — last scored night → explain

The verdict logic: depth is how much light the planet blocks (checked against the published value in σ, using error bars inflated for correlated "red" noise — raw fit errors are always too optimistic); duration deviation catches the partial-coverage trap; O−C (observed minus calculated) is how far the transit arrived from schedule — the actual scientific product; scatter is the night's noise level. ACCURATE needs all of them healthy; NOISY-CONSISTENT means right answer, unusable precision; NON-DETECTION means the transit never showed above the noise.

Model gates → P4 explain

The quality model learns from these scored nights to predict run quality. It never approves anything — it drafts a recommendation a human reviews. We don't trust it on real telescope data until: ≥25 good-quality examples, calibration error (ECE) under 0.10 — meaning "80% confident" is right ~80% of the time — and stable accuracy across three training cycles.
